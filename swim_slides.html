Skill Courses in Higher Education

Writing, data analysis, programming...

Help students get the most bang per buck from skill courses.

Research on learning tells us how.

Practices Backed by Research

  • Outcome-based learning.

    Sharp focus on well-defined goals.

  • Deep learning.

    Problem solving, not memorization.

  • Frequent formative feedback.

    Students do tasks, not quizzes. They get feedback from people, not computers. They get hints for improvements, not just a grade.

  • One-on-one help from knowledgeable people.

    Flipped classes. Teacher as guide, not lecturer.

  • Metacognitive awareness.

    Overcome barriers to learning, e.g., "I don't have the math gene."

  • Other things...

But...

Professors are chemists, mathematicians, economists...

Not trained in learning research.

Fewer than 1 in 10 use learning research in class preparation.

Hard to change.

We would have to change faculty reward systems, hiring practices, graduate student training, workload allocation, administrative priorities, funding models...

Fuggedaboutit!

This level of organizational change is very, very, very difficult.

Solution

Rethink how skill courses are designed and run.

Unbundle professors' jobs.

Let professors just be content area experts. That's difficult enough!

Other people design skill courses.

Course authors are professors with side businesses, practicing professionals, ...

They learn about learning.

Use open source Web software to create courses.

Charge $ to support their work.

The new skill course: Four Roles

Authors create online content, exercises, grading rubrics, ...

Professors choose courses, and customize them.

Students work through online material.

Many hands-on exercises. Write programs, analyze data...

Stuck? Get personal help from professors/assistants.

Graders evaluate student work.

Give students not just a grade, but a list of improvements.

If allowed, students improve their work and resubmit.

Authors

  • Educational entrepreneurs.

    Run their own Web sites, or outsource.

  • Experts in writing, programming, statistics...

    Part-time instructors with Ph.D.s.

    Professors earning summer money.

    Professionals working for themselves.

  • Give away or sell their work.

    Students buy course access, instead of textbooks.

    Authors keep 75% - 100% of the revenue.

    Set low prices, and still make money.

    Can bundle grading services with courses.

Professors

  • Do research.
  • Keep up with their fields.
  • Work with students one-on-one, and in small groups. *
  • Encourage students. *
  • Give final grades.

* High touch

  • Don't lecture.
  • Don't make courses from scratch.
  • Don't need to be experts in both their fields and learning research.

Students

  • Work online mostly.

    Any place, any time.

  • Read content, and do hands-on exercises.

    Get personal feedback from human graders.

  • Help from professors/tutors.

    Optional or required meetings.

    Face-to-face, or online in real time.

  • Encouragement from professors/tutors/graders.

    Email: "You didn't do any exercises this week. Need help?"

Graders

  • Give each student personal feedback for every exercise.
  • Use standardized rubrics created by authors.
  • Can be anywhere in the world.
  • Don't need to be experts. Just:

    Competent (enough).

    Personable.

    Good (enough) writers.

Feature: Course Design Blueprints

CyberCourse blueprints help authors design good courses, before they start writing.

Feature: Learning Maps

Learning maps are course design tools. They help authors visualize (1) the knowledge that students should learn, and (2) the experiences that will help students learn that knowledge.

Feature: Pseudents

Pseudents are pseudo students. They go through the course with the real students. Authors can have pseudents make mistakes, ask questions, do tasks...

Feature: SWIM

The show-what-I-mean (SWIM) editor lets authors focus on content rather than the details of formatting.

Feature: Mobile

CyberCourse formats content to match the display device. Authors can see previews on various devices, if they want.

Feature: Page Order

Authors can change page order with drag-and-drop tools.

Feature: Revision Tracking

CyberCourse tracks changes to each page. This is particularly useful when authors work in teams.

Feature: Workflow Tags

Workflow tags help authors organize tasks. Authors can make up their own tags, like "Check with Dave."

Feature: Feedback (prototype)

The feedback system gives different graders the same standard rubrics. It takes as little as one minute to grade a simple exercise.

Feature: Much More!

Comments, forums, user account control, permissions, themes (look-and-feel), branding, search, media handling, keywords, content cloning, etc., etc.
